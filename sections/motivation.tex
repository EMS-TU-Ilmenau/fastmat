\begin{itemize}
\item linear mappings are present in applied math, physics, engineering, signal processing and data science
\item represented as matrices in finite dimensional spaces
\item many mappings are not arbitrary but follow a certain regularity, logic or physics
\item so they have far fewer degrees of freedom and have a meaning -- they are \textbf{structured}
\item this structure is also present in our minds, our notation, or thinking
\item but a mere rectangle of numbers itself is not able to capture this
\item carrying out the mapping is not a matrix-vector product anymore, but an algorithm on its own, specifically tailored to the mapping
\item we should not describe a rectangle of numbers, but rather the effect of the mapping itself
\item many algorithms only need the mapping and not the whole matrix
\end{itemize}
